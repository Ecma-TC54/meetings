### Discussion

Digital Product Passport (DPP) was introduced as a potentially relevant topic (EU regulatory context, hardware focus) for the TC. Also relevant to Cyber Resilience Act (CRA), extending beyond SBOM, toward “X-BOM” / hardware BOM concepts.

The agreement was to further investigate scope and relevance, capture discussion via GitHub issue and revisit in a future meeting.

Some relevant reference material for the review:

<https://data.europa.eu/en/news-events/news/eus-digital-product-passport-advancing-transparency-and-sustainability>

At its core, a DPP is a digital record linked to a physical product that consolidates verified information on a product's:

* identity and traceability
* materials and composition
* environmental impact
* repair, reuse and recycling guidance
* compliance and safety data

This information is intended to be machine-readable, interoperable and accessible throughout the supply chain — and in some cases to consumers via QR codes or similar technology.”

<https://www.iticp.org/l/eu-digital-product-passports-what-s-new-in-2025-2026/>

A central EU DPP registry and interoperable systems are expected to be operational by mid-2026, laying the data infrastructure foundation.

According to recent commentary from the convenor of a key European working group, eight harmonised standards for the DPP data and interoperability framework are expected to be completed by 2026. These standards will help ensure data consistency, scalability and market-wide compatibility.

<https://untp.unece.org/docs/specification/DigitalProductPassport/>

the DPP is intended to make supply chains more transparent

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EU_Digital_Product_Passport>

<https://untp.unece.org/docs/specification/DigitalProductPassport/#logical-model>

CycloneDX continues to make progress toward the 2.0 release, with significant enhancements to the Hardware BOM, integration of CISA hardware guidance, and the introduction of new capabilities such as defined roles (e.g., manufacturer, supplier) and fine‑grained traceability down to location‑level detail. Additional initiatives include ongoing work on threat‑modeling integration, covering actors, behaviours, and taxonomy, with advances in the Cryptography BOM with refinements based on community feedback, and contributions to the registry.

A new open‑source project, “Attestation Studio,” is also underway to support machine‑readable compliance, evidence, and claims, aligning with broader GRC automation trends and potentially fitting into ENISA and CRA‑related efforts. The Hardware BOM working group remains on track to include its output in 2.0, and the AI/ML BOM work is progressing as well. Notably, CycloneDX continues to gain adoption, with the U.S. government now using it for FIPS certification processes.

### Working Group Updates

#### TG1 – Transparency Exchange API (TEA) (Olle Johansson)

Progress with growing recognition across the ecosystem, including recent references in ENISA’s draft “Security by Design and Default Playbook,” alongside CycloneDX, TC54, and several OWASP projects.

Participation is increasing, though additional active contributors are still needed, and the group is addressing ongoing questions around contributor status.

Current work focuses on strengthening the security architecture and advancing support for transparency and resilience.

#### TG2 – Package URL (PURL) (Philippe Ombredanne)

Steady progress across several areas, including the version range specification and expanded support for non-registry packages such as the Linux kernel and C/C++. Work is also advancing on a new PURL type for non-packaged components, with strong interest from the Linux kernel community and other stakeholders.

VERS continues to progress slowly but according to plan, with a review expected to this TC in early fall. Demand for these developments is growing across open source communities and enterprise internal registries, and the group remains on track for the December standardization cycle.

#### TG3 – Lifecycle / Events (Steve Springett)

Continued progress on lifecycle and event related work, including the development of new event types such as license change events. Collaboration with OASIS OpenEoX remains active, with a jointly authored blog post, featuring Ecma and TC54, expected to be published soon.

#### TG4 – Sustainability (Chris de Almeida and Piotr P. Karwasz)

Work is ongoing on defining project lifecycle states, such as how to distinguish deprecated from superseded components, and how best to capture transitions between them. The group continues to balance usability with necessary complexity, particularly in support of open source due diligence and policy needs. Discussions also remain focused on ensuring that these lifecycle models align with the practical requirements of manufacturers and other stakeholders.
